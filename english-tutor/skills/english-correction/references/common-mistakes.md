# Common English Mistakes for Developers

This reference provides a comprehensive catalog of frequent English errors made by non-native speakers in technical contexts.

## Modal Verbs and Infinitives

### Pattern: Modal + Base Verb

**Common mistake patterns:**
- ❌ "I can helping you"
- ✅ "I can help you"
- ❌ "We should implementing this"
- ✅ "We should implement this"
- ❌ "It will works"
- ✅ "It will work"

**Rule:** After modal verbs (can, could, should, would, will, must, may, might), always use the base form of the verb.

**Modals to watch:**
- can, could
- will, would
- should
- must
- may, might
- shall (rare)

### Pattern: Want/Need/Plan + To + Base Verb

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I want to implementing this feature"
- ✅ "I want to implement this feature"
- ❌ "We need to fixing the bug"
- ✅ "We need to fix the bug"
- ❌ "I plan to deploying tomorrow"
- ✅ "I plan to deploy tomorrow"

**Rule:** After "want to", "need to", "plan to", use the base form (infinitive without "to" again).

## Subject-Verb Agreement

### Third Person Singular

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "The function return a value"
- ✅ "The function returns a value"
- ❌ "This code work fine"
- ✅ "This code works fine"
- ❌ "The API endpoint accept POST requests"
- ✅ "The API endpoint accepts POST requests"

**Rule:** In present tense, third-person singular subjects (he, she, it, the function, the code) require verbs ending in -s or -es.

**Special cases:**
- have → has
- do → does
- go → goes
- try → tries
- catch → catches

### Plural vs. Singular

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "The tests is passing"
- ✅ "The tests are passing"
- ❌ "All functions returns values"
- ✅ "All functions return values"
- ❌ "These data shows that..."
- ✅ "These data show that..." OR "This data shows that..."

**Watch out for:**
- Plural subjects → plural verbs (are, have, do)
- Singular subjects → singular verbs (is, has, does)
- "Data" can be singular or plural depending on context

## Articles (a, an, the)

### When to Use "A/An"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I need create function"
- ✅ "I need to create a function"
- ❌ "This is error"
- ✅ "This is an error"
- ❌ "I found bug"
- ✅ "I found a bug"

**Rule:** Use "a/an" when introducing a singular, countable noun for the first time. Use "an" before vowel sounds.

### When to Use "The"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Let me check a log file" (when there's a specific one)
- ✅ "Let me check the log file"
- ❌ "Please restart a server"
- ✅ "Please restart the server"

**Rule:** Use "the" when referring to something specific or previously mentioned.

### When to Omit Articles

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I'm working on a React"
- ✅ "I'm working on React"
- ❌ "The Python is a great language"
- ✅ "Python is a great language"

**Rule:** Omit articles with:
- Programming language names (Python, JavaScript)
- Framework names (React, Django)
- Technology names in general reference

## Prepositions

### Connect/Login/Deploy

**Common patterns:**
- ✅ "Connect **to** the database"
- ✅ "Log **in** to the system" OR "Log **into** the system"
- ✅ "Deploy **to** production"
- ✅ "Push **to** the repository"

**Not:** "connect with", "deploy on" (in most contexts)

### Work With/On

**Correct usage:**
- ✅ "Work **with** a team" (collaborate)
- ✅ "Work **on** a project" (build/develop)
- ✅ "Work **on** fixing a bug"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Work on a team" (unless literally on top of them!)
- ❌ "Work with a project"

### Different Prepositions

**Correct patterns:**
- ✅ "Integrate **with** the API"
- ✅ "Build **on** top of Node.js"
- ✅ "Based **on** React"
- ✅ "Communicate **with** the server"
- ✅ "Listen **to** events" OR "Listen **for** events"

## Verb Patterns

### Help + Object + Verb

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Can you help me to understand?"
- ✅ "Can you help me understand?"
- ❌ "This helps us to debug"
- ✅ "This helps us debug"

**Rule:** "Help" + object + base verb (no "to")

**Exception:** "Help to" is acceptable without an object:
- ✅ "This helps to debug issues"

### Finish/Stop + Gerund

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I finished to implement the feature"
- ✅ "I finished implementing the feature"
- ❌ "Stop to use this method"
- ✅ "Stop using this method"

**Rule:** After "finish", "stop", "avoid", "consider", use the -ing form (gerund).

### Start/Begin + Infinitive or Gerund

**Both are correct:**
- ✅ "Start implementing" OR "Start to implement"
- ✅ "Begin writing" OR "Begin to write"

**Slight difference:**
- Gerund (-ing) suggests continuation
- Infinitive (to + verb) suggests a new action

## Tense Issues

### Present Perfect vs. Simple Past

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I already fixed the bug" (if just now)
- ✅ "I've already fixed the bug" OR "I already fixed the bug" (if some time ago)
- ❌ "I have fixed it yesterday"
- ✅ "I fixed it yesterday"

**Rule:**
- Use present perfect (have + past participle) for actions with present relevance or unspecified time
- Use simple past for actions at a specific time in the past
- Don't use present perfect with specific time markers (yesterday, last week)

### Future Forms

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I will implemented this"
- ✅ "I will implement this"
- ❌ "I'm going to implementing this"
- ✅ "I'm going to implement this"

**Rules:**
- "Will" + base verb
- "Going to" + base verb

## Word Order

### Adverb Placement

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "I always am writing tests"
- ✅ "I am always writing tests" OR "I always write tests"
- ❌ "I have never seen this error"
- ✅ "I have never seen this error" (correct)

**Rule:** Adverbs of frequency go:
- After "be" verbs: "I am **always** tired"
- Before other verbs: "I **always** write tests"
- Between auxiliary and main verb: "I have **never** seen"

### Question Word Order

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Why this code not working?"
- ✅ "Why is this code not working?"
- ❌ "How I can fix this?"
- ✅ "How can I fix this?"

**Rule:** Questions use auxiliary verb + subject + main verb order.

## Common Confused Words

### It's vs. Its

- **It's** = It is
- **Its** = possessive (belonging to it)

Examples:
- ✅ "It's working" (It is working)
- ✅ "Check its value" (the value belonging to it)

### There/Their/They're

- **There** = location or "there is/are"
- **Their** = possessive (belonging to them)
- **They're** = They are

Examples:
- ✅ "There is a bug"
- ✅ "Their code is good"
- ✅ "They're working on it"

### Your vs. You're

- **Your** = possessive
- **You're** = You are

Examples:
- ✅ "Your code works"
- ✅ "You're right"

### Affect vs. Effect

- **Affect** = verb (to influence)
- **Effect** = noun (result)

Examples:
- ✅ "This affects performance" (verb)
- ✅ "The effect is minimal" (noun)

**Exception:** "Effect" as a verb means "to cause to happen" (rare in technical contexts)

## Technical English Specifics

### API/Endpoint Language

**Correct patterns:**
- ✅ "Call the API"
- ✅ "Make a request to the endpoint"
- ✅ "The endpoint returns JSON"
- ✅ "Send data to the API"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Call to the API" (extra "to")
- ❌ "Make request" (missing "a")

### Error/Exception Language

**Correct patterns:**
- ✅ "Throw an exception"
- ✅ "Catch an error"
- ✅ "Handle the exception"
- ✅ "The error occurs when..."

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Make an error" (use "cause" or "create")
- ❌ "Take an exception" (use "catch")

### Database Language

**Correct patterns:**
- ✅ "Query the database"
- ✅ "Insert a record"
- ✅ "Update the row"
- ✅ "Delete from the table"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Make a query" (use "run" or "execute")
- ❌ "Put a record" (use "insert")

## Register and Formality

### Too Casual

In professional contexts, avoid:
- ❌ "Wanna implement this"
- ✅ "Want to implement this"
- ❌ "Gonna deploy tomorrow"
- ✅ "Going to deploy tomorrow"

### Too Formal

In team communication, these can sound stiff:
- ❌ "I shall implement the feature"
- ✅ "I'll implement the feature"
- ❌ "One might consider..."
- ✅ "You/We might consider..."

**Balance:** Professional but conversational is usually ideal in dev teams.

## Patterns to Master

### "One of the" + Plural Noun

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "One of the function is broken"
- ✅ "One of the functions is broken"

**Rule:** "One of the" requires a plural noun, but the verb is singular (because "one" is the subject).

### "There is/are"

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "There is many bugs"
- ✅ "There are many bugs"
- ❌ "There are a bug"
- ✅ "There is a bug"

**Rule:** Match "is/are" to the noun that follows:
- Singular → "There is"
- Plural → "There are"

### "Make/Do" Collocations

**"Make" collocations:**
- make a decision
- make a change
- make progress
- make a mistake

**"Do" collocations:**
- do work
- do a task
- do testing
- do research

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ "Make work"
- ❌ "Do a decision"

## Quick Reference for Developers

Most common errors to watch for:
1. Modal verbs + base form (not -ing)
2. "Want to" + base form (not -ing)
3. Third-person singular -s
4. "Help" + object + base form (no "to")
5. Articles before singular countable nouns
6. Prepositions (to/on/with patterns)
7. It's vs. its
8. Question word order

Focus on these patterns and most errors will be caught.
